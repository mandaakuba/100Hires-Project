For a long time, one of the first questions people asked about AI models was their knowledge cutoff. But from what we’ve been seeing in testing at Clearscope, that question is becoming less relevant.

Not because models suddenly “know everything,” but because they’re increasingly looking things up before answering. In many cases, what shows up in an answer isn’t coming from training alone—it’s coming from what the system retrieves in real time.

And that changes the dynamic quite a bit.

It means new information can influence answers much faster than people expect. It also means visibility isn’t gated by the next model release anymore. Instead, it’s shaped by whether your content is part of the set the model chooses to read.

We’ve been seeing pretty consistent patterns here, especially in how different systems approach retrieval vs. recall. We wrote up a deeper breakdown of what’s going on and why knowledge cutoffs are starting to matter less.

For a long time, we thought the search engine wars were over. As you know, they aren't. They just moved.

We’ve been running a series of tests on how AI systems actually generate answers, and a few things became very clear: The “search universe” is smaller than people think.

In one test, a single 100-response control run covered 74–100% of queries that showed up over the next 7 days. Not just similar queries. The same ones. That  means you can map most of what a model will retrieve surprisingly quickly.

At the same time, the systems aren’t static. In Gemini, 20–29% new queries still emerged over a week, especially for research and fresh intent.

So the surface evolves, but inside a bounded space. And then there’s the part most people keep missing: Not all intent behaves the same.

Commercial queries are the most stable. The same brands show up over and over again. But fresh/trending queries are the opposite—high churn, low consistency.

So what looks like chaos on the surface is actually pretty structured underneath. Which leads to a bigger shift: You’re not competing for rankings anymore. You’re competing to be included in the set of sources the model pulls from.

Because if you’re not in that set you don’t exist in the answer at all.

Most conversations about “influencing AI” focus on the wrong layer.

People talk about prompts, brand mentions, or trying to “train the model.” But those things sit after the real decision has already been made.

The real question is simpler: How does the system retrieve information before it answers?

Some models search the web constantly. Others mostly rely on their existing knowledge and search only occasionally. That difference alone determines how often new content has the opportunity to shape what the AI says.

Influence doesn’t start with the answer. It starts with the retrieval behavior behind it.
